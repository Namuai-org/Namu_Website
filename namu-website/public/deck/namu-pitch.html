Users & business model
Free for people. Paid where scale and reliability matter.

Who uses the platform — we don’t charge them

Everyday users (always free)

Anyone using Hausa AI-Studio to write, plan, learn, or work — students, farmers, shop owners, creators, and professionals in Nigeria, Niger, and the diaspora. Our job is to make them more creative, productive, and efficient in their language, not to monetize their seat.

Why free at the edge

Mass adoption builds trust, feedback, and the data flywheel that makes our models worth paying for elsewhere — without putting a paywall on community use.

How Namu makes money (long run)

◇
Open models, hosted API — we open-source our models; as quality improves, we monetize managed inference and API usage (SLAs, scale, compliance) for organizations that can’t self-host.
◇
Who pays — NGOs, national and international companies, corporates, and government programs that need reliable African-language AI in products, services, and citizen-facing systems.
◇
Volume = revenue — pricing tracks API demand as more institutions integrate Hausa (then other languages) into workflows, support lines, and public services.

Path: ship a free, loved consumer workspace → ship strong open models → capture enterprise and institutional API demand.

Market opportunity
$16.5B
Industry analysis projects Africa’s AI market at roughly $16.5B by 2030, up from on the order of $4.5B in 2025 — strong tailwinds as enterprises and consumers on the continent adopt AI.
Our opportunity: own the language-native layer for African communities — starting with the Hausa-speaking belt (Nigeria, Niger, diaspora), then the same playbook for other languages. The workspace stays free for users; revenue scales with API usage from NGOs, government, and companies as our open models mature and institutions need hosted, dependable access at scale.
